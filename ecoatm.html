Mobile App and Point of Sale (POS) Optimization Using Data and Process Improvement Strategies

Overview

ecoATM is an industry-defining company that buys and sells used smartphones under two company names. Its dominant portal is a physical kiosk that can be found in most domestic Walmart, Dollar General, and Kroger stores. These kiosks were my main design responsibility. I also contributed to the company’s two mobile apps and two websites.

Goals While in My Role:

  1. Work with the Product department to streamline the estimate process on the kiosk
  2. Develop personas and products for new target market segments
  3. Utilize the user-centered design process to identify the reasons for app uninstalls (then decrease them)

Goal 1:

In researching smartphone recycling kiosks around the world (ecoATM is the only domestic provider), I determined that the company would have trouble competing with them on transaction speed. After seeing a lunchtime webinar about what the R&D department had done during the pandemic to limit physical contact with the kiosk, I asked to use their new technology, which scanned the smartphone’s face to acquire its specs, to streamline the estimate process. After a few iterations, the design moved forward to development and QA.

Outcome:

By working with the R&D department, I was able to reduce the number of screens in the estimate process by 38% while increasing the accuracy of the information we received, thus improving the accuracy of our price quotes (and our customers’ experiences).

Goal 2:

Before I arrived at ecoATM, the company had paid a consultant to describe current customers and identify new target markets. This meant that we had silhouettes of the people I was trying to delight. My department and I used the consultant templates to flesh out the personas with names, pictures, etc. However, when I reached out to our previously siloed In-Store Rep and Business Insights teams, I learned that our consultants’ data didn’t include theirs. In the end, analysis of the two data sets concluded similar things, but the internal data set was larger and had more detailed gender and age numbers. No one before me in the UX department had ventured to understand the customer from these teams’ perspectives. Based on what I found in my exploration and newfound partnership with diverse internal teams, we changed the “current user” persona to female. (And it didn’t cost anything).

Outcome:
  1. Communicating with internal teams gave me better information about our typical users than did the expensive consultants my company hired. Simply by talking to previously overlooked internal resources, I discovered data-backed insights that the company had just paid an outside firm to gather. In the end, the augmented data and insights allowed me to make our personas more accurate—and effective.
  2. Having target personas in mind, especially when we were doing the Dollar Promo, inspired experimental kiosk screen designs. For example, we tested this screen with the recycling icon to keep our environmentally focused persona interacting with the kiosk even when we could not pay for their phones. The Champion screen in this Champion-Challenger scenario did not mention recycling. When the Champion design, which did not mention recycling, proved more effective, we determined that the market was more aligned with the cash-oriented persona.

Goal 3:

When I downloaded then opened the ecoATM app on my phone, it crashed my phone. Three times. Bug one: it didn’t like Pixels? Androids? What’s the problem? I noted it for QA. When the app finally loaded and stayed functional for a while, it required that I agree to the Terms & Conditions immediately, before I (the anonymous user) even knew what the app was about. And if I didn’t agree, the app closed on me! Opportunity for improvement number two. I worked with the Legal department to compromise on a place for the T&C’s further along in the process and proposed a secondary user path that did not require agreeing at all (but that politely informed the user that we could not personalize their experience if they did not).

I also worked on the design, replacing simple, black-and-white text with pictures of phones in different states on the condition-description page (something that was previously hidden behind an information icon).

I recommended defaulting to the map of other available kiosks (instead of a written list) to enrich the visual appeal of the page and, surprisingly, cut down on loading time.

Outcome:
  1. My fresh eyes on a legacy product generated a lot of new ideas for efficiency and value without requiring a lot of new coding. I combined findings from the previously mentioned kiosk consultants with a new approach to the app’s basic functions and style. The resulting proposal replaced a single-function app limited to those willing to sign away all rights on the landing page with a multifunctional tool that integrated marketing, an account dashboard, and two paths to an estimate.
